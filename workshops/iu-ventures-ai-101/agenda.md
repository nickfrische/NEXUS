## 11:15 - 12:15 | Phase 2: Research, Context, and Claude Projects (60 min)

**Goal:** Each person understands how to use AI for real research and sets up a Project for their actual work.

**Flow:**

**Research with AI -- the money demo (25 min)**

This is Olivia's moment. Set it up this way:

"Olivia, you told us diligence reports take you 15-20 hours per week. I want to time something. We're going to paste in everything publicly available about Motion Sports -- the platform Anthony Leal and his IU co-founders launched in September 2024 -- and see how far we get on a first-pass diligence draft in 10 minutes."

Use this prompt live:

"I am the Executive Director of the IU Angel Network, Indiana University's angel investment vehicle. I am doing early-stage diligence on Motion Sports Inc., a college athletics platform co-founded by IU basketball alum Anthony Leal. Based on the information below plus any additional research you can find online, produce the first draft of a 10-12 page diligence report. Cover: what they do and for whom, the market size and growth dynamics, the competitive landscape and how Motion is differentiated, team background, current traction and customer list, risks and questions to ask the founders, and areas where more information is needed. Cite sources for any external data. Flag clearly where I should verify or fill in with additional materials."

Then paste in what is publicly available (IU Ventures press release, Anthony Leal news coverage, any Motion website info).

Run it on screen. Watch what it produces. Discuss together: what is good, what needs Olivia's judgment, where does she need to add information from the founder's materials.

Then ask the room: "What just happened to 15-20 hours per week?"

**Giving AI the right context (10 min)**
Walk through the context template from the Phase 1 guide. Have each person write 3-4 sentences about who they are and what they do. This becomes the foundation for their Project instructions.

**Claude Projects setup (25 min)**

Walk through what Projects are: the binder you hand a new analyst on day one, except it loads automatically every time.

Live setup -- each person builds their first Project:

- Tony: "IU Ventures CEO Office" -- store his role, his investment thesis, his funds, his key portfolio context. Instructions tell Claude to always write from the perspective of a 20-year veteran of Indiana's entrepreneurship ecosystem who cares about keeping talent and capital in the state.
- Jon: "IU Founders and Funders Network" -- store his role, the network structure, the summit details, examples of alumni intro emails that have worked. Instructions tell Claude to always match his warm-professional voice.
- Olivia: "IU Angel Network Diligence" -- upload her diligence template, her investment criteria, examples of past reports. Instructions tell Claude to always cite sources, flag what is missing, and never invent financial figures.

Each person asks one question inside their new Project and sees the difference.

**Facilitator notes:**
- Have Motion Sports info pre-pulled before the session so the demo is instant, not fumbling.
- Olivia's diligence demo is the most important moment of the day for her. Do not rush it. Let the output sit on screen for a moment. Let the room react.
- Tony will watch Olivia's demo carefully. He manages the funds that need the diligence done. If it impresses her, it impresses him.

## 1:45 - 2:45 | Phase 3: Planning and Strategy with AI (60 min)

**Goal:** Each person uses AI as a thought partner on something real.

**The reframe (5 min):**
AI is not just a task executor. It is a thinking partner. You can use it to structure a plan, stress-test an idea, anticipate problems, and organize your thinking before you start working. This is one of the highest-leverage uses most people never try.

**Flow:**

**Demo: AI as a strategic partner (10 min)**

Show how "think out loud with me" prompting works. Example: paste in the IU Ventures summary of goals for 2026 and ask Claude to identify the three biggest operational bottlenecks standing between where they are and where they want to be, then suggest what category of tools would help.

**Each person plans something real (35 min)**

Tailored to each person:

Tony: Use AI to structure a brief for his team on what an AI-enabled IU Ventures looks like in 12 months. What does the Fellows Program look like? What does the portfolio company support function look like? What does alumni communications look like? Let him drive. Let AI organize his thinking into something he could share internally.

Jon: Build the newsletter content plan for the next 3 months leading up to and through the Venture Summit. What goes out in April, May, and post-summit? Use AI to generate the content pillars and draft the May summit preview. If time allows, draft the summit recap email template now so it is ready the moment the summit ends.

Olivia: Use AI to build a reusable diligence checklist template she can apply to every new company. Structured by section: team, market, product, financials, risks, open questions. Then use that template as the foundation for her Projects system. Every future diligence conversation starts from this baseline.

**Skills and automations: the final unlock (15 min)**

Once someone has a context layer and a workspace, the next step is skills -- pre-built automations that run a full workflow with a single command.

A skill is a set of instructions Claude stores and can run on demand. Instead of typing the same sequence of steps every time you do a recurring task, you build the skill once and invoke it with one line.

Examples for this room:

Jon types `/alumni-intro [name1] and [name2]`. Claude reads both alumni files, identifies the best connection angle, drafts the introduction in Jon's voice, and formats it ready to send. What took 15 manual minutes runs in under 2 minutes -- and sounds exactly like him every time.

Olivia types `/first-pass-diligence` and attaches a founder deck. Claude runs the full research sequence: market analysis, competitive landscape, team background, traction assessment, risk flags -- structured to her exact template and standards. She gets a first draft in minutes instead of hours.

Tony types `/deal-brief [company name]`. Claude pulls the relevant emails, the fund history, the relationship context, and surfaces everything he needs before a founder call -- without him digging through anything.

Skills are not complicated to build. They are plain-language instructions saved as a file in the workspace. Phil builds these with each person in Phase 4. The point to understand now: the skills work because the context layer is already there. The context is what makes them smart. Without it, a skill is just a template. With it, a skill is a real workflow.

**Share and discuss (10 min)**
Each person shows what they built. What surprised them. What still needs their judgment.

## 3:00 - 4:00 | Phase 4: Organization and People Tracking (60 min)

**Goal:** Each person leaves with a working contact or investment tracker, connected to AI.

**The setup (5 min):**

All three of them manage a large number of relationships. Tony has a portfolio of 25+ active companies plus a global alumni network. Jon manages 2,500+ identified founders and funders plus the alumni base. Olivia screens 10-15 companies monthly and manages ~100 angel investors. None of them have a system that is connected to AI.

Today changes that for at least one part of their work.

**Tool choice by person:**

Olivia is already in Airtable. Go deep there. Show her how to structure a proper investment tracking base with portfolio companies, pipeline deals, and investor members in separate tables with linked views.

Jon: Notion is the best fit for him. He manages relationships and content simultaneously. A Notion page per network node or per alumni intro request, with AI summarizing each conversation. Simple to start, powerful to build on.

Tony: Follow his lead. He manages at a high level. If he is interested in something specific, go there. If he is watching and advising, let him. His buy-in matters more than his build.

**Build the tracker (35 min)**
Each person builds or refines their tracker with AI helping. Use AI to generate the columns, the structure, and the first few entries.

Prompt example for Olivia: "I am the Executive Director of a university angel investment network. I track two types of things: portfolio companies I have already invested in, and new companies in my pipeline that I am actively evaluating. Build me an Airtable structure with the fields I need for each type. For portfolio companies include: company name, founder names, sector, investment date, round, check size, current status, last touchpoint, and next action. For pipeline companies include: company name, stage, referral source, current diligence status, next step, and due date."

Prompt example for Jon: "I manage a network of 2,500+ IU-affiliated founders, funders, and operators. I want to track my key relationships and make sure no one goes cold. Build me a simple Notion database template with these fields: name, role, organization, relationship context (how we know each other), last touchpoint, next action, and priority level (superstar / warm / keep warm)."

**Populate with real contacts (10 min)**
Each person adds 3-5 real names to their tracker. Use AI to write the context notes: "Based on these notes from my last meeting with [name], write a 2-sentence relationship summary I can store in my CRM."
