## The four parts of a good prompt

| Part | What it means | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **Who you are** | Tell Claude your role and context | "I am the Executive Director of IU Angel Network, a university angel investment fund..." |
| **What you want** | Be specific about the task | "...write a first-pass overview of this company for our investment committee." |
| **What you have** | Tell it what materials to use | "I am attaching their pitch deck and a news article about them." |
| **How you want it** | Format, length, tone, constraints | "Keep it under two pages. Cite sources. Flag anything you are uncertain about." |

---

## Weak vs. strong -- side by side

| Weak prompt | Strong prompt |
|-------------|---------------|
| "Write an email to a founder." | "I am a VC at IU Ventures following up with a founder after a first meeting. The meeting went well. Write a warm, brief email that confirms our interest and asks for their data room. Keep it under 150 words." |
| "Summarize this company." | "I am evaluating this company for seed investment. Summarize what they do, who they sell to, and the top three risks I should ask about. Keep it to half a page." |
| "Draft an intro email." | "I am introducing two IU alumni. Sarah is a healthcare founder in Indianapolis. Mark runs an early-stage fund focused on Midwest operators. Write a two-paragraph intro that names the specific reason these two should talk." |

## Three habits that separate good results from great ones

**1. Ask how to approach it before you start**
Before any big task, ask: *"What is the best approach to this? What do people usually miss?"*
Then start the actual work once you have a framework.

**2. Ask for options before you commit**
Instead of asking for one answer, ask: *"Give me three ways to approach this and the tradeoffs of each. I will pick one."*

**3. Plan before Claude acts**
For anything complex: *"Walk me through your full approach first -- then wait for my input before you start."*
This keeps you in control before work is done, not after.

---

## Phrases that work

- *"Before you start, tell me how you would approach this."*
- *"Here is what I know. What am I missing?"*
- *"Give me a first draft -- I will edit from there."*
- *"Flag anything you are not sure about rather than guessing."*
- *"Ask me any questions you need before you begin."*
- *"Here is some context about me: [your role, your org, what you are working on]"*

---

## One rule that matters most for this room

**Never let Claude invent numbers.**

Always include this in any research or analysis prompt:
> *"Cite your sources. If you are not sure about a figure, say so. Do not fabricate statistics."*
