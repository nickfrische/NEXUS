### Healthcare Vertical Discussion

**Phil's family background:** Dad = cardiovascular surgeon (IU/Methodist → U of Florida). Grandfather = cardiovascular surgeon. Mom = OT. Phil started as neuroscience major. The healthcare world is second nature to him but he'd always ruled it out because university hospitals are notoriously slow.

**Phil's observation that triggered the call:** Visited his parents' hospital over Easter. AI being used in research (disease detection, etc.) but ZERO adoption for workplace efficiency. Wondered if private practice was more business-savvy and thus a better target.

**Ryan's verdict on the healthcare landscape:**

| Segment | Ryan's Take |
|---|---|
| University hospitals / non-profits | Too slow. No competitive pressure to get efficient. They make money regardless. "They can be inefficient because they can be." |
| Large health systems | Same problem — institutional inertia. Resultant (big consulting firm) confirmed same issue. |
| Private practice (independent) | Shrinking fast. PE is buying them up. Hard to target a disappearing market. |
| PE-backed private practice networks | More business-savvy. Buying up independent practices, forming mini-systems (like St. Vincent). Better option. |

**Key Ryan quote:**
> *"If AI works best where there's lots of data that's not well-analyzed and lots of inefficiencies, it'd be hard to do better than healthcare. However, they don't have competitive forces forcing them to get efficient."*

**The PE trend (confirmed by Ryan):**
- Optum (United Healthcare) and others are major buyers of private practices
- John Ryan (FIJI house corp president) was CEO at Ortho Indy — he did this exact model (merged Fort Wayne ortho + Evansville ortho → now "Ortho Indiana"). Same concept as PE rollups but doctor-owned.
- Days of truly independent private practice are numbered — and diminishing quickly

**Why private practice is disappearing:**
1. Doctors are trained by employed docs → they do the same thing
2. Technology is expensive and complicated to scale alone
3. Heavy regulations
4. Zero med school training on running a business
5. Generational shift — younger doctors don't want the grind of private practice ownership
6. Employed docs have better benefits, no call coverage stress, health insurance handled
7. Employed groups negotiate far better insurance reimbursement rates
